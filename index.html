How is the Internet Infrastructure Connected into Real Life?



In our interconnected world, the sight of networks and internet infrastructure has become a common yet remarkable aspect of modern life. Like hidden arteries coursing beneath the surface or delicately suspended above, cables and wires weave intricate patterns, carrying the digital lifeblood that fuels our daily existence.

From the towering cell towers and antennas that bridge distances wirelessly to the unassuming manholes concealing the secrets of underground connectivity, these visible manifestations of the internet infrastructure remind us of the invisible web that binds us all together. As we navigate our surroundings, the subtle presence of these technologies serves as a visual testament to humanity's insatiable quest for connectivity and the wonders of the digital age.







Cables in Wellington

Internet connectivity is primarily facilitated through a network of cables that span vast distances both underground and above ground. These cables carry data in the form of electrical signals or light pulses. Fiber-optic cables are increasingly used for their high-speed data transmission capabilities. These cables are often bundled together and laid out across cities and regions, forming the backbone of the internet infrastructure.

External Termination Box

Example

External termination boxes, also known as external termination points (ETPs) or network interface devices (NIDs), are devices used in telecommunications and internet installations to connect the service provider's network to the customer's premises. They serve as an interface between the external communication lines and the internal wiring within a building or property.

Example

These termination boxes are typically installed on the external walls of homes, offices, or other buildings, allowing easy access for technicians and minimizing the need to enter the premises for maintenance or repairs. They are designed to protect the connection points and ensure the smooth flow of communication signals.

Cabinets in New Zealand

Cabinets connect our copper and fibre network from the local exchange and distribute it to each street in the neighbourhood using underground or aerial cables.
